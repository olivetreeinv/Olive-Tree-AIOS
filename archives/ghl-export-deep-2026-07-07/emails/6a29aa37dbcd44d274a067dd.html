The Next Multifamily Opportunity Isn't Where Everyone's Looking

→ Why Overlooked Submarkets with Strong Fundamentals Are Attracting Investor Attention as New Development Slows

Zoning constraints in certain submarkets mean zero new supply. Weak demand in others means supply shouldn't exist. The difference is everything.

Here's what institutional capital is doing right now:

They're not competing in Miami, Austin, Phoenix (headline markets flooded with new supply).

They're buying in South Bay (CA). Ventura County. Hillsborough (OR). Lake Oswego. Louisville South Central.

Why? Supply constraints + high occupancy (94%+) + durable demand = pricing power.

Compare:

  • Miami: High new supply, 1.2% rent growth, extreme competition

  • South Bay: Zoning limits supply, 2.3% rent growth, minimal competition

What makes a submarket a winner:

✅ Strong employment bases
✅ High occupancy (94%+)
✅ Limited new supply from 2020-2024
✅ Supply constraints have a reason (zoning/geography, not weak demand)

Avoid:

❌ New supply in pipeline despite low occupancy
❌ Zoning changes coming that unlock development
❌ Falling occupancy + new supply arriving

The takeaway:

The next multifamily winners aren't in the markets getting all the capital. They're in the supply-constrained submarkets nobody's competing for yet.
