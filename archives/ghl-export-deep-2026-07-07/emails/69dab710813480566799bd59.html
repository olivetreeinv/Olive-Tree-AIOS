Where America Is Moving—and Why It Matters for Investors

→ How Shifting Migration Patterns Are Quietly Reshaping Rental Demand and Long-Term Investment Strategy

The Midwest hasn't added more domestic migrants than it lost in years.

That changed in the 12 months through June 2025.

The region joined the South as the only major U.S. regions posting net domestic migration gains—a shift driven by affordability and two resurgent state capitals.

Columbus, OH, and Lansing, MI, both flipped from net domestic migration losses to gains, helping tip the regional balance.

A few other trends stand out:

Carolina metros are surging

Myrtle Beach, SC, grew 3.2% in the latest period—the fastest pace in the Carolinas. Spartanburg, SC, and Wilmington, NC, also posted above-average gains driven almost entirely by domestic movers.

Urban cores are losing ground to suburbs

Central counties of the 53 largest U.S. metros grew just 0.3%, while their suburban counties grew at three times that rate.

Small and mid-sized metros outpaced both.

Immigration's role is shrinking

A significant slowdown in international migration has made domestic movement the primary driver of population change.

That puts even more weight on affordability as the deciding factor for where people land.

Two-thirds of U.S. counties now record more deaths than births, up from fewer than half as recently as 2019.

Translation: Domestic migration is now the dominant force shaping where rental demand builds next.

What this means for multifamily investors:

The growth markets aren't the obvious ones anymore.

Columbus, OH. Lansing, MI. Myrtle Beach, SC. Spartanburg. Wilmington, NC.

These aren't the cities everyone talks about. That's exactly why they matter.

Lower entry costs. Rising population. Growing rental demand. Less competition from institutional capital chasing the same tired coastal plays.

The strategic play:

Follow migration, not headlines.

Coastal primaries are losing domestic population. Midwest and South secondary markets are gaining it.

Suburban counties are growing 3x faster than urban cores.

If your investment thesis is still "buy in the biggest cities," you're following yesterday's migration map.

Today's map shows affordability driving the decisions. People are moving where housing costs work with their income.

That's your signal.

🏠 Should You Rent Your Home If It Doesn't Sell?

→ What Homeowners Need to Consider Before Becoming an Accidental Landlord

When your house sits on the market longer than expected, frustration builds.

You start asking: what now?

And for a growing number of homeowners, that turns into: should I just rent it instead?

While it sounds like a simple backup plan, becoming an "accidental landlord" is actually a much bigger decision than most people realize.

Why the number of accidental landlords is rising:

According to Zillow, 2.3% of homes available for rent were previously listed for sale.

That may not sound like a lot, but it's the highest share in almost 6 years.

If you're faced with the same choice, here's what you need to know.

Ask yourself these 3 questions first:

1. Would your house actually work as a rental?

What's right for your situation depends on location, your home's condition, and what the rental market looks like in your area.

Think about:

  • If you're moving away, how will you handle ongoing maintenance and repairs from a distance?

  • Does your house need repairs before it's rental-ready? Do you have the time, energy, and funds for that?

  • What's the rental market like in your area? Are there vacancies?

  • What monthly rent could you realistically expect?

C&C Property Management explains: "At the heart of any rental market is the balance between supply and demand. When more tenants are looking for housing than there are available units, rental prices rise. On the other hand, if new construction adds hundreds of apartments or homes to a neighborhood, prices can soften."

If your home would struggle to stand out or command the rent you need, that's something to take seriously.

2. Are you ready to be a landlord?

This is the part people don't always think about upfront.

On paper, renting sounds like passive income. In reality, it's a hands-on responsibility.

Imagine:

  • Midnight calls about clogged toilets or broken air conditioners

  • Chasing down missed rent payments

  • Covering unexpected repairs

  • Fixing damage between tenants

Those costs hit when you least expect them.

3. Have you run the real numbers?

Renting out your house comes with extra expenses. Here are a few of the biggest according to Bankrate:

  • Higher insurance premiums (landlord insurance typically costs about 25% more)

  • Management fees (property managers typically charge around 10% of rent)

  • Routine maintenance and services

  • Advertising fees to find tenants

  • Gaps between tenants, where you cover the mortgage without rental income

For some people, that's totally manageable. For others, it's more than they want to take on.

Your next step: Talk to your agent first

Before you make any decision, talk to your agent about overhauling your sales strategy first.

Sometimes it's not that buyers aren't out there. It's that something about the pricing, presentation, or marketing isn't lining up with what they're looking for.

A few small adjustments can make a big difference.

The reality:

Renting can be a great choice for the right person with the right house.

But if you're only considering it because your listing didn't get traction, there may be a better solution.
