Don't Judge a Home by How Long It's Been Listed

→ Why Longer Market Time Can Signal Opportunity, Not Warning

There's this unspoken rule in real estate: If it's been sitting, something's wrong.

Buyers scroll past anything over 30 days. "If it was good, it would've sold."

But here's what's actually happening:

Longer days on market doesn't equal damaged goods. It equals negotiating room.

Three reasons homes sit longer—and why you should care:

Reason 1: Testing the waters went wrong

Seller priced aggressively. Market said no. Now? They're adjusting. You're walking into a motivated conversation, not a problem property.

Reason 2: It's different, not defective

Unique layout. Unconventional style. Smaller buyer pool doesn't mean lower quality—it means less competition when you're the right fit.

Reason 3: Timing killed momentum

Holiday listing. Weather delays. Life circumstances. The house didn't change. The calendar did.

Where the opportunity lives:

Fresh listings spark bidding wars. You're one of twelve offers. Price goes above ask.

A home at 45 days? You're one of two offers. Seller's thinking about next steps. You have leverage.

What to look for:

  • Recent price drops (reality setting in)

  • Life event sellers (divorce, estate, job transfer)

  • Seasonal listings (December/January victims)

If the bones are solid and the only issue is time or price—not foundation or location—you just found your advantage.

The contrarian play:

Next time you're filtering listings, remove the "days on market" limit.

You might be filtering out the best negotiating position you'll get all year.

🏠 Large Multifamily Units Take Over the Market

→ What Record-High Market Share Means for Investors in 2026

Something fundamental shifted in multifamily.

Large units—2-bedrooms and bigger—now hold 60% of the rental market. That's not a trend. That's a complete market restructuring.

If your investment thesis is still built around studios and 1-bedrooms, you're playing a game that already ended.

What drove the flip:

🏠 Life got bigger

Millennials hit their 30s and 40s. Families formed. Remote work turned extra bedrooms from luxury to necessity.

The "22-year-old in a studio" renter? That cohort is shrinking, not growing.

📊 Market share breakdown:

  • 2-bedroom units: 35% (was 28% in 2020)

  • 3+ bedroom units: 25% (was 18% in 2020)

  • Studios + 1-bedrooms: 40% (was 54% in 2020)

That's a 14-point swing in six years. In market terms, that's an earthquake.

💰 The profitability math:

"But studios get higher rent per square foot!"

True. Also irrelevant.

Here's what actually matters:

900 sq ft 2BR at $2.45/sq ft = $2,205/month
500 sq ft studio at $2.85/sq ft = $1,425/month

The 2BR generates 55% more monthly income on similar operational footprint.

Add this: Families stay 2-3 years. Young professionals cycle out in 12-18 months.

Fewer turnovers = lower vacancy loss = lower capex churn = higher NOI.

The math isn't close.

🗺️ Geography matters more now:

This shift isn't uniform. It's concentrated.

Secondary markets are dominating:

  • Boise, Idaho

  • Des Moines, Iowa

  • Greenville, South Carolina

  • Fort Wayne, Indiana

These cities offer space at affordable rents. They're absorbing families priced out of coastal primaries.

Meanwhile, NYC and SF still support studio/1BR demand—but the growth velocity is in 2BR+ units in the heartland.

What this means for your portfolio:

If you're holding small units: Evaluate repositioning opportunities. Can you combine units? Reconfigure layouts? Upgrade finishes to command family-level rents?

If you're buying: Target value-add 2BR+ properties in secondary markets. Look for good bones, tired interiors, family-oriented neighborhoods.

If you're developing: The unit mix debate is over. 2BR+ is the future. Build accordingly.

The takeaway:

Market preferences changed. Supply is catching up. Investors clinging to small-unit strategies are betting against demographic reality.

2026 is the year to realign. Go where the demand actually lives.
