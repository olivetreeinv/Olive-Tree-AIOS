## Summary
Kenneth argues that cash-secured puts don't always make sense as part of the wheel strategy, using Joby Aviation as a case study where poor premium-to-capital-ratio and unfavorable risk/reward profiles make alternative capital deployment more efficient for income generation.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Don't sell cash-secured puts when premium-to-capital ratio is poor (e.g., $3,600 capital for $10-20 premium)
2. Use Fibonacci retracements to identify potential downside targets before committing to cash-secured puts
3. If selling 2 cash-secured puts at a strike yields only $90 premium on $3,600 capital, redeploy that capital to covered calls on higher-premium opportunities
4. Avoid cash-secured puts on stocks with massive runups (high volatility/retracement risk) when premium doesn't justify the capital lockup
5. Time the market: wait for stocks to drop to Fibonacci support levels, then buy shares and sell covered calls instead of selling puts preemptively
6. Premium threshold rule: $90 on $3,600 (2.5% return) is not a good use of capital—seek higher-return opportunities
