## Summary
Kenneth Suna discusses why he dislikes international and emerging market ETFs, comparing their underperformance (10-20% over 10 years) to U.S. indices like the S&P 500 (200% over 10 years). He argues that U.S.-based companies already provide international exposure and warns against buying international funds after recent price surges due to trade war rhetoric, cautioning against becoming a bag holder.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. S&P 500 has ~480 of 500 companies with international exposure, providing diversification without direct international ETF purchases
2. International ETFs underperform U.S. investments dramatically: 10-20% growth over 10 years vs. S&P 500's ~200% and tech ETFs' ~500%
3. Major companies like Coca-Cola, Apple, and Nvidia generate 50%+ of revenue from international consumers, giving U.S. investors indirect international exposure
4. VXUS shows 28% year-to-date gains but only 60% over 10 years; EM emerging markets ETF up 33% YTD but only 57% in 10 years—suggesting growth is priced in
5. Rare earth ETF (VANC) up 78% YTD but only 44% in 10 years; CQQ (China QQQ) up 47% YTD but only 59% in 10 years—recent rallies suggest valuation risk
6. Consider international allocations as small speculative positions (not 60-100%) after major price moves, with plans to add on pullbacks rather than chase gains
