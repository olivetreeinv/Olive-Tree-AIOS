Sometimes being an options trader is just about knowing when to take advantage of a really great opportunity. Many years ago, I bought thousands of shares in Ford. I'm talking about 2008 housing crisis back when it was like $2 a share. And as it's been climbing back up slowly, they reintroduced their dividend. That was great. And I've been selling off some shares because the growth has been kind of slow. I also have a pretty large position, like several hundred shares at 16 per share. And over the month of May, Ford has just absolutely skyrocketed. Look, it was $12 and then here we go, up to 16. I'm finally at break even. Now, I could have just sold the shares at 16 bucks and broken even, gotten my money back. But I said, "Let me see if I can get paid to sell my shares." Because Ford stock had just ripped over the last few weeks, I knew that the options premiums would be really high. And I had a hunch that the stock would maybe pull back, meaning I might not get assigned and could continue selling covered calls in the future. Since my plan is to eventually sell the shares anyway, when the time comes that it gets assigned, bye-bye. Last week, I sold six contracts. You can see the little red flag right there. They expire today. And you can see that it is under $16. My strike price was 16, so I get to keep my shares. Plus I made, let's see here, where is it? I made $302. I get to keep that money. I get to keep the premium and my shares, which means next week, if Ford experiences more volatility in their stock price and the options premiums increase, I can sell another couple contracts, make a little bit more money. Because the stock price declined, the momentum has fizzled and the options premiums for next week are not very lucrative. You can see that the $16 one is 20 cents. So, 20 * 6 is $120. Not a whole lot, but still something. Well, that's how I can get paid to sell my shares and even make money even though I break even on the stock price. Pretty cool.
