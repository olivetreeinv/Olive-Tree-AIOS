## Summary
This document covers the foundational framework for building and scaling an apartment syndication company, with practical guidance on deal structure types, sponsor/investor equity splits, fee schedules, underwriting metrics, CapEx sequencing, preferred financing strategies for value-add deals, and market selection criteria. It is written as a reference sheet for operators who are sourcing, underwriting, and capitalizing multifamily acquisitions.

## Key Takeaways
1. **Three pillars of an apartment company are Deal, Money, and Execution** — operators must identify their core competency and partner or hire to fill the other two.
2. **Value-add deal structure targets 15–25% annual returns** using a 90/10 LP/GP equity split, 8% preferred return, and 70/30 cash flow split post-pref, with a 50/50 promote above a 15% IRR hurdle.
3. **Total closing costs run 3–4% of purchase price**, inclusive of lender fees, capital raise fees, title, escrow, inspections, legal, cost seg, acquisition fee, and working capital — this must be modeled into underwriting from day one.
4. **The 1% Rule, 75% Rule, 8%+ CoC by Year 3–4, and 15–20% IRR are the four go/no-go filters** for any acquisition candidate.
5. **CapEx sequencing matters**: deferred maintenance first, upgrades second, vacant units before occupied, then set a turn calendar — this protects basis and accelerates stabilization.
6. **Bridge financing is the preferred debt structure for value-add deals**, allowing acquisition, renovation, and stabilization before refinancing into agency debt (Freddie/Fannie) within 3–4 years.
7. **For every $1 in CapEx spend, target a $1.20–$1.30 rent bump** (20–30% rent lift per dollar invested) to validate capital improvement assumptions.
8. **Market selection criteria**: 1%+ population growth, 2%+ job growth, 3%+ rent growth, $56K+ median income, diverse employers including Fortune 500 companies.
9. **Purchase price discipline**: stay at or under 10x NOI (implied ~10% cap) or a 20:1 NOI-to-value ratio at a 5% cap rate — use this as a quick sanity check on asking price.
10. **Working capital reserve** should equal 1–2 months of gross revenue and must be included in total project costs at closing.

## Action Items
Things to apply directly to Olive Tree deals or process:

- **Add a fee schedule line-item checklist to the underwriting model**: acquisition fee (1–3%), property management (3–5%), asset management (1–2% of gross income), construction/reno management (5–8%), capital raise fee (3%), working capital (1–2 months gross revenue), and loan fees (1% lender + 0.5% broker).
- **Validate every deal against the four quick filters before building a full model**: 1% Rule, 75% Rule, 8%+ CoC by Year 3–4, 15–20%+ project IRR.
- **Use the $1 CapEx → $1.20–$1.30 rent bump benchmark** to stress-test renovation scope assumptions on value-add acquisitions.
- **Confirm CapEx sequencing in every business plan**: deferred maintenance → upgrades → vacant units first → occupied unit turn calendar.
- **Model bridge-to-agency debt path on all value-add deals**: bridge at ~36-month I/O, then agency refi into Freddie/Fannie at stabilization (Year 3–4).
- **Apply the 10x NOI cap as a ceiling discipline** when evaluating asking prices against current or pro forma NOI.
- **Check target markets against the six market metrics scorecard** (population growth, job growth, rent growth, university systems, median income, employer diversity) before advancing any deal to full underwriting.
- **Build the 15% IRR hurdle / 50/50 promote structure** into all LP/GP waterfall models as the standard promote trigger above preferred return.
- **Use 90/10 LP/GP equity split as the default syndication structure** for value-add deals when setting up the capital stack.

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Preferred Return (Pref) | A minimum annual return paid to LP investors before the GP receives any profit split; typically 6–8% on value-add deals. |
| Promote | The GP's disproportionate share of profits above the preferred return; also called carried interest or the "promote structure." |
| Hurdle Rate | An IRR threshold (typically 15%) above which the profit split shifts from 70/30 to 50/50 in favor of the GP. |
| LP (Limited Partner) | Passive equity investor(s) contributing 90% of equity in a typical syndication structure. |
| GP (General Partner) | The sponsor/operator contributing 10% of equity, signing on the loan, and managing the deal. |
| Acquisition Fee | A one-time fee paid to the GP at closing, typically 1–3% of the purchase price, to compensate for deal sourcing and underwriting. |
| Asset Management Fee | An ongoing fee paid to the GP, typically 1–2% of gross income, for overseeing the investment. |
| Capital Raise Fee | A fee charged for raising investor equity, typically 3% of total capital raised. |
| Working Capital Reserve | A liquidity buffer equal to 1–2 months of gross revenue, held at closing to cover early operational shortfalls. |
| Bridge Financing | Short-term, interest-only debt used to acquire and renovate a value-add property before stabilization; typically 36 months with extension options. |
| Agency Debt | Long-term permanent financing through Freddie Mac or Fannie Mae; the target exit from bridge financing after stabilization. |
| 1% Rule | A quick filter: average monthly projected rent per unit should exceed 1% of the purchase price per unit. |
| 75% Rule | All-in project costs (purchase + rehab) should be less than 75% of the expected stabilized/after-rehab value. |
| Cash-on-Cash (CoC) Return | Annual pre-tax cash flow divided by total equity invested; target 8%+ by Year 3–4 post-stabilization. |
| IRR (Internal Rate of Return) | The annualized return on invested capital accounting for the timing of all cash flows; target 15–20%+ over a 5-year hold. |
| 10x NOI Rule | A quick valuation check: purchase price should ideally be no more than 10 times the property's NOI. |
| 20:1 NOI-to-Value Ratio | At a 5% cap rate, $1 of NOI creates $20 of asset value — used to estimate value creation from NOI improvement. |
| CapEx (Capital Expenditures) | Physical improvement costs invested into the property; target 20–30% rent bump for every $1 spent. |
| Deferred Maintenance | Repairs and upkeep that have been postponed; should be addressed first in any CapEx plan before aesthetic upgrades. |
| Cost Segregation | An IRS-approved tax strategy that accelerates depreciation on components of a building to reduce taxable income; included in closing costs. |
| General Conditions Fee | A construction fee covering project overhead, typically 7–8% of hard costs. |
| Builder Fee | The general contractor's profit margin, typically 5–6% of hard costs. |
| Developer Fee | A fee for projects requiring entitlement work, typically 3% of total project cost. |
