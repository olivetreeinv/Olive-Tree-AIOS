## Summary
This document is a draft Accredited Investor Verification Letter (Appendix B) prepared for TBD LLC, a California LLC raising $3,250,000 through the sale of 65 Class A Preferred Non-Voting Membership Units at $50,000 per unit. The letter outlines the legal requirements for verifying investor accreditation status under Rule 506(c) of Regulation D (Securities Act of 1933), as updated in September 2013. It provides two qualification tracks — Natural Person (income test or net worth test) and Entity — and specifies the supporting documentation investors must submit, including tax forms, brokerage statements, credit reports, or third-party verification letters from licensed professionals.

## Key Takeaways
1. Under Rule 506(c), the issuer (sponsor) bears an affirmative obligation to **take reasonable steps to verify** that each investor is an Accredited Investor — self-certification alone is no longer sufficient for this exemption.
2. The **income test** requires $200,000 individual income (or $300,000 joint with spouse/spousal equivalent) in each of the two most recent years, with a reasonable expectation of the same in the current year; documentation includes W-2s, 1099s, K-1s, or filed Form 1040s.
3. The **net worth test** requires $1,000,000 net worth (individual or joint), explicitly **excluding** the primary residence's fair market value from assets and requiring that any mortgage debt in excess of the home's FMV be counted as a liability.
4. Investors must submit supporting documentation within **10 days** of signing the verification letter.
5. The issuer may present investor information to legal, accounting, and financial advisors **without prior notice**, but must give prior notice before disclosing to other parties.
6. The company retains sole discretion to **refuse any subscription** for any reason, regardless of accreditation status.
7. Incremental mortgage debt taken on the primary residence within **60 days prior to closing** could affect net worth calculations and must be disclosed to the company promptly.
8. Third-party verification (registered broker-dealer, SEC-registered investment adviser, licensed attorney, or CPA) is an accepted alternative to submitting raw financial documents.

## Action Items
Things to apply directly to Olive Tree deals or process:
- **Add an Accredited Investor Verification Letter** (modeled on this template) to Olive Tree's standard investor onboarding package for any 506(c) offering.
- **Do not rely on investor self-certification alone** for 506(c) raises; build a checklist requiring W-2s/1099s/K-1s or a third-party verification letter before accepting a subscription.
- **Track the 60-day mortgage window** for net worth investors — include a covenant in the verification letter requiring prompt notice of any new debt secured by the primary residence before closing.
- **Set a 10-day documentation deadline** in the subscription workflow; flag and follow up on any investor who has not delivered supporting docs within that window.
- **Offer the third-party verification path** as the default for high-net-worth investors who are uncomfortable sharing personal financial statements directly.
- **Redaction is permitted** — remind investors they may redact Social Security numbers and other non-essential personally identifiable information from submitted documents.
- **Confirm investor qualification category** (natural person vs. entity) at first contact so the correct section of the verification letter is pre-populated before sending.

## Key Terms
| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| Accredited Investor | An investor meeting the income or net worth thresholds in Rule 501(a) of Regulation D; eligible to participate in unregistered private securities offerings |
| Rule 506(c) | A Regulation D exemption that permits general solicitation of investors, provided the issuer takes reasonable steps to verify that all purchasers are Accredited Investors |
| Rule 501(a) | The SEC rule that defines "Accredited Investor" for purposes of Regulation D |
| Income Test | Accreditation standard requiring $200,000 individual (or $300,000 joint) income in each of the two prior years with expectation of the same in the current year |
| Net Worth Test | Accreditation standard requiring $1,000,000 net worth (excluding primary residence equity), individually or jointly with a spouse or spousal equivalent |
| Spousal Equivalent | A cohabitant occupying a relationship generally equivalent to a spouse, as defined in Rule 501(j) under the Securities Act |
| Primary Residence Exclusion | The estimated FMV of a primary residence is excluded from assets in the net worth calculation; mortgage debt up to FMV is also excluded, but any excess mortgage debt is counted as a liability |
| Third-Party Verification | Written confirmation of accredited status provided by a registered broker-dealer, SEC-registered investment adviser, licensed attorney, or CPA, in lieu of submitting raw financial documents |
| Class A Preferred Non-Voting Membership Units | Equity interests in the LLC that carry preferential economic rights but no voting rights in company governance |
| Confidential Offering Memorandum (COM) | The private placement document describing the terms of the offering, business plan, and risk factors; equivalent to a PPM (Private Placement Memorandum) |
| Regulation D | SEC regulations providing exemptions from the registration requirements of the Securities Act of 1933 for certain private offerings |
| Subscription | A prospective investor's formal offer to purchase units in the offering; not binding until accepted by the company |
