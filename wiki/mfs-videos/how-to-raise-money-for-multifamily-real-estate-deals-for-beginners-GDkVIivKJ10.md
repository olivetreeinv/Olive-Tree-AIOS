## Summary
Todd Robinson, a syndication attorney, discusses legal structures for raising capital in multifamily real estate, including SEC compliance (506b/506c offerings), PPM documentation, and deal structuring. While focused on capital-raising mechanics, the conversation includes broader market observations about 2025-2026 multifamily conditions but lacks Brian's specific deal sourcing and underwriting challenges.

## Key Takeaways
1. 506b offerings allow up to 35 unaccredited investors with pre-existing substantive relationships (three-touch rule: met in person, had coffee, sent email); 506c offerings require accreditation but allow advertising
2. Attorney-drafted contracts (not broker contracts) protect buyers on reps/warranties, rent-ready credits (make-ready units), and collection contingencies before closing
3. Never accept broker contracts; insist on attorney-drafted contracts with minimum 3-page addendum covering conditions, reps/warranties, and rent-ready credits for vacant units
4. Tie rent rolls to bank deposit statements for minimum 6 months; request delinquency and eviction lists to verify stated economic occupancy (collections, not physical occupancy)
5. Syndication attorney fees: $12,500 flat fee for PPM/SEC filings; $15,000-$25,000 for transactional work on 15-50 unit deals; larger deals ($50M+) run $50,000-$80,000 with opinion letters
6. Property management: Keep units vacant rather than lease bad tenants; vacancy + turnover costs ($2-3K per unit) + eviction process + loss rent = worse than 1-2 month vacancy
7. To raise capital: start with friends/family (print LinkedIn/Facebook contacts), host dinner parties to establish pre-existing relationships for 506b compliance, avoid pitching directly
8. By 2026, supply/demand imbalance will compress as construction stops; rents will rebound but interest rates likely to stay 5-7% (normal), requiring sponsors to buy right and create value on revenue side
