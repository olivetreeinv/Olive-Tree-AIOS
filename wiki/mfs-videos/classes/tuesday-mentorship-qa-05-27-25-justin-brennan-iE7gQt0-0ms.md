## Summary
Justin Brennan analyzed two multifamily deals live: a 12-unit value-add in Miami and a 20-unit stabilized property in Huntsville, Alabama. He walked through underwriting mechanics, cap rate analysis, renovation budgeting, and investor return structures using his deal analyzer tool.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Use 5-10% vacancy assumption even on stabilized properties to stress-test and identify true market rents—when you hit that vacancy threshold, you know you're at market rent and running the business correctly
2. For value-add deals, aim to force appreciation by improving the cap rate 2 points from acquisition (e.g., buy at 6 cap, improve to 8 cap, then sell at 5.5-6 cap)
3. On stabilized properties with no major renovations, moving the cap rate up 1-2 points through operational improvements is sufficient
4. Use minimum 1.25 debt service coverage ratio as the hard floor—if numbers don't hit that, back into the price by reducing purchase offer until DCR works
5. For a 20-unit stabilized property, budget 6-8% blended management fee (5% base + leasing/commission fees), not the 5% broker's estimate
6. Set GP acquisition fee at 2-3% of purchase price, but confirm actual closing costs (title, points, lender fees) fit within total closing cost budget
7. Build investor returns with waterfall: preferred return (7% example) until met at 100%, then split equity 70/30 LP/GP; explain simply as annual cash flow percentage plus future appreciation at sale/refi
8. For renovation budgets on 12-unit 1-bed/studio: exterior work ~$50k, interior improvements ~$120k total (under $10k/unit with appliances ~$2k, flooring ~$2k), targeting $130+ monthly rent bumps
9. Stress-test deals by adjusting expense assumptions upward—if OM shows unusually low expenses, add back management fees and taxes to get realistic 40-50% expense ratio
10. Agency debt available for loans over $1M with 90%+ occupancy on 1980s-era product at 6.5% or lower rates
