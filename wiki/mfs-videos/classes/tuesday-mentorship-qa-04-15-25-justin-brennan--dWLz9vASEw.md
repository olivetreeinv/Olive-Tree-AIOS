## Summary
Justin Brennan mentors multifamily investors on deal underwriting, due diligence, and capital raising. Key focus includes evaluating seller responses to 22-question due diligence checklists, understanding representations and warranties in contracts, and structuring syndication with proper legal documentation to avoid money pits and legal liability.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Send 22-question due diligence checklist to seller BEFORE going into contract to validate property condition, roof age, plumbing/electrical systems, Section 8 units, water heater specs, and wiring type (verify copper vs aluminum by having inspector remove outlets/fixtures)
2. Trust but verify: cross-reference seller's answers against lease audits and inspections; aluminum wiring fraud occurred 3x in their deals despite sellers claiming copper
3. Properties built pre-1980 (especially 1960s-1970s) have higher maintenance costs due to cast iron piping, aluminum wiring, flat roofs; 1980+ is quality threshold with ABS/PVC lines and copper electrical
4. Shut-off valve testing critical: failure to inspect all building water shut-offs individually caused $25k in remediation when half didn't work, forcing total complex shutdowns that cause 2-3 additional leaks over 3 months due to air/sediment in old piping
5. For flat roofs with prior leak history, inspect heavily and use repair/credit leverage to renegotiate; roof age 20+ years requires professional inspection
6. First deal sweet spot is 10-30 units with $300-600k capital raise; 12-unit deal with 6-7% closing costs should cover legal/syndication fees; deal economics pay for SEC documentation (~$14k first set, ~$7k thereafter)
7. Real estate professional designation (500-700 hours/year managing real estate) allows pass-through of negative K-1 losses against W-2 ordinary income, not just capital gains—applies to multifamily syndication AND short-term rentals via bonus depreciation
8. Cost segregation study ($5-7k) financially justified only at scale (10+ units minimum); applies to any asset type but only makes sense when spread across sufficient units
