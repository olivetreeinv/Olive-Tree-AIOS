## Summary
Justin Brennan walks through live analysis of a 43-unit Fort Worth multifamily property listed at $5M (seller asking $4M), teaching how to identify distressed seller motivation through loan analysis, verify suspicious financials, and underwrite deals to actual market value rather than asking price. Key insight: the seller bought in Feb 2023 for $2.6M with a likely 3-year note coming due in Feb 2026, creating forced-sale pressure that justifies aggressive underwriting.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Commercial loans are 3, 5, 7, or 10-year terms (not 30-year like residential) - amortization schedule is separate from loan maturity date
2. Always ask sellers: 'Is your loan assumable?' and 'What's your current debt balance?' to find loan records in county data or Real Estate Radar software
3. Request last 12 months of bank statements to verify T12/P&L - owner-provided financials are unreliable; use bank statements for 'trust but verify'
4. Don't send detailed due diligence questions until LOI is signed/inked - brokers won't respond to questions before contract phase
5. Apply minimum 45% expense ratio to any owner-provided revenue numbers for properties of this age/type to back-calculate actual NOI
6. Expect Section 8 cap at max 10% of units per your portfolio - banks will ask this question and high percentages create operational/legal risk
7. For distressed sellers showing equity loss: they face three options: (1) refinance with bank if willing, (2) sell at loss to avoid foreclosure, or (3) hand keys back to bank - use this framework in negotiations
8. Track new lease spikes (question: how many new leases in last 90 days?) - large recent spike indicates owner filling vacancy before sale, shifting tenant risk to buyer
