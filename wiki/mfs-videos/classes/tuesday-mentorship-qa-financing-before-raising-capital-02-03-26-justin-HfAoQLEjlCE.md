## Summary
Justin Brennan conducts a mentorship Q&A focused on financing before raising capital. He reviews due diligence checklists and underwriting documents with mentees, analyzes two specific multifamily deals (16-unit Lansing MI and 28-unit Louisville KY), and provides detailed guidance on deal structuring, loan assumptions, expense modeling, and lender pre-approval letters needed for LOIs.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Separate property taxes and insurance from loan payments in underwriting—these are operational expenses that must be listed separately, not wrapped into PITI
2. Use the 75% break-even metric: if 25%+ of units go vacant, you should still cover all expenses and mortgage payments
3. For underwriting benchmarks: 1% rule, 75% rule, IRR analysis, and check if property relies on appreciation or cash flow
4. Section 8 subsidy is better when 70% subsidized (tenant pays 30%) vs 100% paid—subsidy travels with tenant, giving them incentive to maintain unit and keep rental assistance
5. Due diligence period should be 28-30 days minimum, not 2 weeks—insufficient time to review all leases, financials, inspections, and secure investor commitments
6. Request lender quote on official letterhead stating loan amount contingent on standard conditions—frame it as 'lender quote' not 'pre-approval' to reduce lender friction
7. Closing cost estimate should include $10K for legal (not lower), plus standard appraisal, environmental, title, insurance, escrow for 6 months
8. Operating reserves: plug 50K capex reserve minimum for older properties to reduce equity requirement; renovations typically 11K per unit for 28-unit example
