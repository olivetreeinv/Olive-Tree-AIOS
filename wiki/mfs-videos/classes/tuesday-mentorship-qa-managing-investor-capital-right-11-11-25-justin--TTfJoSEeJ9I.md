## Summary
Justin Brennan walks through critical underwriting errors found in a multifamily deal, specifically discrepancies in the broker's repair & maintenance line items and how these errors cascade through the financial model, ultimately affecting cap rate, debt service coverage ratio, and deal viability. He teaches the community how to audit broker P&Ls for hidden one-time expenses and validate operating expense ratios against industry benchmarks.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Repairs & maintenance should average $800/unit/year for standard operating costs; turnover costs average $1,500/unit with 25-30% annual turnover = ~$13,500-$15,000 for a 30-unit property
2. Never buy a rental property when cap rate is more than 0.5 points below your loan interest rate (e.g., 4.99% cap on 6.25% loan does not work)
3. For property age benchmarks: 1980+ older = 45-50% expense ratio; 1980-2010 = 35-45%; 2010+ newer = 30-40%
4. Projected investor returns should be quoted simply as 17-20% annualized (cash flow + refinance/sale proceeds), not just cash-on-cash which is typically 8-10%
5. Preferred return (6-8%) paid to investors first quarterly before general partner gets profit splits (70/30, 60/40, 50/50, or 80/20)
6. When you invest 10% as GP you 'double dip': earning LP returns on your capital PLUS general partnership promote/tip on profits
7. Extract one-time capital expenses (e.g., 14 water heaters replaced) from P&L line items; broker's "cleaned" numbers often mask the true recurring operating costs—demand actual T12 work orders and accounting detail to verify
