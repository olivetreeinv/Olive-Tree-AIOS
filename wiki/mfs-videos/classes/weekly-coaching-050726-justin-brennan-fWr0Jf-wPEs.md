## Summary
Justin Brennan coaches on multifamily deal underwriting through two detailed case studies: Julian's closed 23-unit deal in Michigan ($1.15M final price after $150K price reduction via due diligence), and Jason's Cincinnati 31-unit prospect. Key focus areas include financial due diligence red flags, proper expense modeling, and capital raise execution.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Use $600-$800 per unit for repairs & maintenance on pre-1980 buildings; don't accept artificially low numbers from sellers
2. Request insurance quotes from vendors upfront (quote process takes 2-3 days) rather than relying on seller's rates that may reflect master policies you cannot access
3. Property taxes and insurance are the two largest expense line items—verify both independently; use AI tools for tax verification
4. Demand to see non-operational expenses line item on P&L; if seller refuses, it signals hidden R&M costs being misclassified
5. For 1950-1980 vintage properties, expect 10-12% economic vacancy/credit loss even in 93% occupied scenarios; use historical data to justify
6. Ask directly for commitment after presenting: 'Are we doing $50K or $100K?' vs. pause; isolate objections into three buckets: timing/capital, trust/comfort, or deal quality
7. Get insurance quotes early at $400-$600/unit baseline for market; flood insurance can add $5,000-$25,000/year—verify before LOI
8. Build 10-15 LOIs in pipeline; don't be too selective; broker conversations and seller motivation will determine which land
9. Extension options critical: Julian used 30-day extension (90 days total vs. standard 60) when capital raise tightened—ensure contracts allow this
10. Verify rent rolls show actual current rents for occupied units; separate vacant units into line items and assume market rent for underwriting purposes
