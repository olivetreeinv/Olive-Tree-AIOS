## Summary
Justin Brennan mentors Brian Norton's AIOS members through a detailed underwriting review of a 23-unit multifamily property in Cleveland, OH. The discussion covers critical deal-analysis fundamentals including property tax calculations, expense verification, unit-mix conversions, and the mathematical relationship between NOI and property valuation. While the specific Cleveland deal may not match Brian's target markets (GA/TN/AL), the underwriting principles and mental framework are directly applicable to his 15-50 unit value-add deals.

---

## Key Takeaways
1. Use ChatGPT to determine county-specific property tax assessment methods and rates rather than assuming broker numbers, which are often incorrect by $15,000+
2. Verify expense line items skeptically: for a 1926 building with 23 units, maintenance/repairs of only $1,800 is unrealistic; use formula of 23 units × 30% vacancy = 7 units × $1,500 = ~$10,000 as baseline
3. For stabilized properties with mixed government tenants (CHA/Eden), underwrite vacancy/credit loss at 5-7% rather than relying on OM data
4. Management fees of 5-6% are standard, but verify the property management company has proper systems, infrastructure, and team before accepting lower rates
5. For properties with minimal obvious renovations, budget $25,000-$50,000 capex reserve from day one rather than inflated amounts
6. When sending aggressive LOIs, call the broker immediately and say: 'It's based on numbers I'm looking at—I'd love to go over it with you and see where maybe I'm wrong' to position yourself as collaborative, not adversarial
7. Use the income approach to valuation: every $100/month rent increase per unit on a 10-unit property = $220,000 in added value at a 5.5% cap rate, demonstrating the power of operational improvements over market comps
