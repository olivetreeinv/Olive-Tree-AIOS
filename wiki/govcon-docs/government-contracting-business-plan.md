## Summary

This document, based on a Koerner Office Podcast episode with government contracting practitioner Natalie, outlines a complete federal subcontracting business model operable with near-zero startup costs and no industry-specific expertise. The model works as follows: register an LLC and a free SAM.gov account, identify federal solicitations on SAM.gov for services you can source, cold-call subcontractors in the relevant geography for quotes, mark up those quotes, submit a proposal, and collect the spread when the government pays net 30. Natalie generates $10,000–$12,000/month in net profit working roughly 1–2 hours per week maintaining active contracts and a few additional hours per week prospecting new ones. Her largest single contract was a $962,000 five-year landscaping award in Colorado against a subcontractor cost of $700,000 — a $262,000 gross profit over the contract term. This model maps directly onto Olive Tree's existing federal subcontracting pipeline.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The government is legally required to spend with small businesses.** Federal small-business set-aside spending runs into the hundreds of billions annually. Olive Tree's LLC structure already positions it to compete for these contracts without additional licensing or industry credentials.
2. **Industry expertise is not required — process expertise is.** The evaluator model, proposal structure, and contract lifecycle are consistent across NAICS codes. Once Olive Tree learns the process for one contract type (e.g., janitorial/561720), the same workflow applies to landscaping, catering, hazardous waste, relocation services, and beyond.
3. **Contracts under $350,000 require no past performance.** RFQ (Request for Quote) submissions under this threshold are effectively open to any registered SAM.gov entity with a competitive price and a responsive proposal. This is Olive Tree's lowest-friction entry zone.
4. **AI dramatically compresses proposal writing time.** Natalie cited AI assistance as a key time-reducer for proposal assembly. Olive Tree should develop a standardized AI-assisted proposal template per NAICS category.
5. **~8–9% of contracts receive zero bids.** These represent near-automatic win opportunities. Filtering SAM.gov for low-competition or no-bid categories should be a standing weekly prospecting task.
6. **The margin structure is wide.** Natalie's documented margins range from ~47% markup (formalin: $700 → $1,500/pickup) to ~27% (Idaho catering: $41K → $52K) to ~37% (Colorado landscaping: $700K → $962K). Olive Tree should target a minimum 20% gross margin as a bid floor.
7. **LLC naming strategy affects evaluator perception.** A generic, industry-neutral business name (e.g., "Olive Tree Services LLC" or "Olive Tree Professional Services") prevents evaluator bias when bidding across unrelated NAICS codes.
8. **Value-add is a legal requirement — not optional.** Pure pass-through is illegal. Olive Tree must document its coordination, compliance management, invoicing, and communication functions as genuine contract value in every subcontracting relationship.
9. **Payment is reliable and formulaic.** Federal government pays net 30 post-invoice. Purchases under ~$15,000 can be paid immediately via government purchase card. Cash flow is highly predictable compared to commercial work.
10. **Hit rate improves materially with repetition.** Natalie improved from 1/14 (7%) to 4/9 (44%) as she refined her targeting and proposal quality. Volume of bids submitted is the primary early lever.

## Action Items

*Directly applicable to Olive Tree's federal subcontracting model:*

- [ ] **Confirm SAM.gov registration is active and current.** Verify Olive Tree's LLC entity is registered, UEI is active, and all NAICS codes relevant to current and target bid categories are listed in the SAM.gov profile.
- [ ] **Audit Olive Tree's LLC name for cross-NAICS neutrality.** If the registered DBA implies a specific industry, evaluate whether a neutral DBA (e.g., "Olive Tree Services Group") would reduce evaluator bias on non-core bids.
- [ ] **Add a weekly SAM.gov prospecting block (2 hours).** Filter for: (a) service contracts under $350,000 (no past performance required), (b) NAICS codes where Olive Tree has or can source subcontractors, (c) solicitations with few or no existing bids.
- [ ] **Build a subcontractor cold-call script and tracking sheet.** For each new bid category, standardize the outreach process: Google search → phone call → quote request → quote log. Track sub name, contact, NAICS, state, quote amount, and response time in the [[govcon-subs/]] directory.
- [ ] **Establish a minimum bid margin rule: 20% gross above sub quote.** Use this as a hard floor. Do not submit proposals where the sub quote + overhead leaves less than 20% gross. Document this in Olive Tree's bid pricing SOP.
- [ ] **Develop an AI-assisted proposal template per NAICS category.** Start with Olive Tree's most active NAICS code (e.g., [[govcon-bids/laurel-river-lake-janitorial]] → 561720). Build a reusable prompt and template that populates scope, pricing, past performance (if required), and value-add language automatically.
- [ ] **Create a past performance library.** Log every won and performed contract (including any teaming partner past performance) so Olive Tree can satisfy the >$350,000 past performance requirement and pursue larger contracts over time.
- [ ] **Document Olive Tree's value-add activities per contract.** For each active subcontract, maintain a brief log of coordination touchpoints, compliance checks, and communication actions. This protects against pass-through scrutiny and supports past performance narratives.
- [ ] **Target the $0-bid and low-bid SAM.gov segments weekly.** Run a standing search filter for solicitations with 0–2 existing offers. These are Olive Tree's highest-probability wins. Log findings in [[govcon-bids/]] even if not immediately pursued.
- [ ] **Track bid hit rate from day one.** Record every bid submitted, outcome, and NAICS code. Goal: replicate Natalie's improvement curve from ~7% to ~44% win rate within 12–18 months of consistent bidding volume.

## Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| SAM.gov | System for Award Management — free federal registry where contractors must register to bid on government contracts |
| RFQ | Request for Quote — solicitation type typically used for contracts under $350,000; no past performance required to respond |
| RFP | Request for Proposal — solicitation type for larger or more complex contracts; may require past performance documentation |
| NAICS Code | North American Industry Classification System — 6-digit code categorizing the type of work; determines which set-aside categories apply |
| Set-Aside | Contract reserved exclusively for small businesses (or sub-categories: women-owned, veteran-owned, 8(a), HUBZone, etc.) |
| Past Performance | Documentation of prior contract execution; required for contracts over ~$350,000; can be satisfied via teaming partner |
| Teaming Partner | An established contractor whose past performance Olive Tree can reference in proposals above $350K, in exchange for a role on the contract |
| Net 30 | Standard federal payment term — government pays 30 days after invoice submission and service confirmation |
| UEI | Unique Entity Identifier — replaces the old DUNS number; assigned during SAM.gov registration; required on all federal bids |
| Pass-Through Scheme | Illegal arrangement where the contract holder adds no value and simply routes work and payment to a subcontractor without oversight or coordination |
| Price Ceiling | The maximum the government will pay for a contract; often discoverable from prior award data on USASpending.gov |
| Government Purchase Card | Federal agency credit card used for small purchases (~under $15,000); enables near-immediate payment rather than net 30 |
| Spread / Margin | The difference between what the government pays Olive Tree and what Olive Tree pays its subcontractor — this is Olive Tree's gross profit |
| Solicitation | The government's formal notice of a contracting opportunity, posted on SAM.gov with scope, deadline, and evaluation criteria |
